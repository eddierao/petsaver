Core Lesson
Why This Matters Right Now
Lost pets are more common than most people realize.

About 10 million dogs and cats are lost in the US every year
1 in 3 pets will get lost at some point in their life
Most lost pets are found within 2 to 3 miles of home
The first 24 hours are the most important for getting a pet back
Most families don't know where to start when their pet goes missing
This is happening on real streets near you, this week.

What Saves a Pet vs. What Loses One
The difference between reunion and heartbreak is often one thing: a fast post seen by neighbors.

Lost pet outcomes Recovery rate
Dogs reported quickly to the community ~93%
Cats reported quickly to the community ~75%
Dogs in a shelter with no ID tag or chip 15%
Cats in a shelter with no ID tag or chip 2%
Most recoveries happen because a neighbor saw the post
47% of pets are found through direct community help
Without a fast, public post, a lost pet can end up in a shelter and never come home
Important
Your site is the bridge between "my pet is missing" and "a neighbor just spotted them."

Why a Lost Pet Finder Works
A simple, fast page beats anything a panicking owner can pull together alone.

Lost Dogs of America's Facebook network has helped reunite 75,000+ dogs
Most owners post in the wrong places or wait too long
A clear page lets neighbors share in one tap
A photo + last-seen spot is all most reunions need
A small site can be shared across whole neighborhoods in minutes
Your job this project: build a small site that turns a missing pet post into something a whole neighborhood can act on.

What Is a PRD?
A PRD is a short plan for what you are building.

A good PRD answers four questions:

Who is the site for?
What problem does it solve?
What sections must be on the page?
What can wait until later?
Think of a PRD like the sketch an architect draws before a house is built.

What Is an MVP?
An MVP is the smallest version that is clear and useful enough to review.

An MVP should feel like:

a rough drawing, not the final painting
a first draft you can show a reader
a working page, not a polished site
The MVP is not the end. It is the first version you can get honest feedback on.

AI as Your Thinking Partner
Claude is more than a code writer. Claude can be a thinking partner who helps you decide what your product should be.

The 3-move pattern you will use all course:

Share your idea -- tell Claude what you are thinking
Ask Claude to push back -- question you, not agree with you
Refine together -- use the pushback to sharpen your decisions
If Claude only agrees with you, your prompt was too soft. Ask it to challenge you.

How to Structure a Good Prompt
A strong prompt has four parts.

Part What It Says
Who you are A Grade 6 student, a beginner, your goal
What you want A PRD, 5 questions, a sketch, a list
How to help you Ask questions, push back, keep it short
What NOT to do Don't answer for me, don't skip ahead
Weak prompt: "Help me with my PRD."

Strong prompt: "I am a Grade 6 student. Ask me 5 questions, one at a time, to help me pick one audience and one problem for my site. Do not answer for me."

What Counts as MVP in This Project?
For this project, your MVP is not "just anything that opens."

Your Lesson 7 MVP should include:

one working page
a clear title and intro
a Recently Reported Lost Pets section
a What To Do If Your Pet Is Missing section
a How to Help a Neighbor's Lost Pet section
a final call to action (report a lost pet / share the page)
What Can Wait Until After the MVP?
Some ideas are worth saving for later.

Examples:

a checklist
a short quiz
myth vs fact cards
visual polish and stronger layout
Challenge 1: Discovery Conversation With Claude
Note
Reference: Intro & Setup

Steps:

Open Claude at claude.ai or in VS Code.

Copy this discovery prompt. Change anything in brackets to match you:

I am a Grade 4 student planning a lost pet finder website for a class project.

Before I write anything down, ask me 5 short questions, one at a time,
that will help me pick ONE real audience and ONE clear problem my site can solve.

Wait for my answer before you ask the next question.
Do not answer the questions for me.
Answer each question in one short sentence.

After all 5 questions, ask Claude: "Based on my answers, who is my site for and what problem does it solve?"

Write down the one audience and one problem you agreed on.

Tip
If Claude only agrees with you, reply: "Push back on my idea. What feels weak or too broad?"

Note

Based on the audience and problem we just discussed,

My PRD must answer:
